Mr. Sunshine: The Sunset of Joseon and the Dawn of Resistance

In the global records of cinematic excellence, the 2018 series Mr. Sunshine stands as an untouchable titan. Written by the legendary Kim Eun-sook, the drama is set during the turbulent years leading up to the Japanese occupation of Korea. The story follows Eugene Choi (Lee Byung-hun), a man born into slavery who escapes to the United States and returns to Joseon as a Marine Corps officer. However, his mission is complicated by his encounter with Go Ae-shin (Kim Tae-ri), a noblewoman who leads a double life as a sniper for the Righteous Army. At iQPeak, we analyze this narrative as a profound exploration of national sovereignty and the heavy cost of patriotism in an era of colonial expansion.

Geopolitical Friction: The Five Powers in Joseon

The success of Mr. Sunshine lies in its meticulous historical intelligence. The drama portrays Joseon not just as a setting, but as a chessboard where five global powers—the United States, Japan, Russia, China, and the fading Joseon monarchy—clash for dominance. Eugene Choi represents the external perspective, caught between his identity as an American soldier and his roots as a Joseon native. The narrative depth is further enriched by characters like Goo Dong-mae (Yoo Yeon-seok), a butcher's son turned Japanese samurai, and Kudo Hina (Kim Min-jung), the powerful owner of Hotel Glory. Each character represents a different strategic response to the loss of a nation.

The Righteous Army and Guerilla Warfare

From a strategic perspective, the portrayal of the Righteous Army (Uibyeong) is a masterclass in resistance storytelling. Instead of grand, organized armies, we see commoners—potters, blacksmiths, and noblewomen—engaging in guerilla warfare to protect their sovereign right to exist. The cinematography by Lee Eung-bok captures the stark contrast between the modernization of Seoul (Hanseong) and the rugged, untouched mountains where the resistance breathes.
